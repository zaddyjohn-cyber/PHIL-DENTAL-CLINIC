Dental Emergency in Lagos? Here's Exactly What to Do

Emergency Care · April 2, 2026 · 6 min read

A sudden, severe toothache or dental injury can be alarming — and in Lagos, knowing exactly who to call and what to do in the meantime can make the difference between saving and losing a tooth. This guide covers the most common dental emergencies and the first-aid steps to take while you get to a dentist.

What Counts as a Dental Emergency?

Not every dental problem needs same-day attention, but the following situations should be treated urgently — delay can turn a treatable problem into a much bigger (and more expensive) one:

  • Severe, unrelenting toothache that keeps you awake or prevents eating
  • Knocked-out (avulsed) tooth
  • Cracked or broken tooth with sharp pain
  • Dental abscess (visible swelling, severe pain, foul taste in mouth)
  • Lost or broken crown or filling with sensitivity or pain
  • Soft tissue injuries — bitten tongue, cut lip, bleeding gums after trauma

Severe Toothache: What to Do Right Now

Toothache at this level is almost always a sign of infection or nerve damage — neither will resolve on its own. While you arrange to see a dentist:

  • Take over-the-counter pain relief (ibuprofen is more effective than paracetamol for dental pain)
  • Do not place aspirin directly on the tooth or gum — this can cause chemical burns
  • Rinse gently with warm salty water to reduce bacterial load
  • Avoid very hot or very cold food and drink which can intensify nerve pain
  • Call your dentist — do not wait to see if it improves on its own

Knocked-Out Tooth: You Have 30–60 Minutes

A completely knocked-out adult tooth can sometimes be reimplanted successfully — but timing is critical. Here's what to do:

  1. Handle by the crown (top), never the root — touching the root damages the cells needed for reimplantation.
  2. Rinse gently with clean water if dirty — do not scrub.
  3. Keep it moist — place the tooth back in the socket if possible, or hold it in the cheek (between cheek and lower gum) or in a cup of whole milk.
  4. Get to a dentist within 30–60 minutes — this is the window during which successful reimplantation is most likely.

Dental Abscess: This Is a Medical Urgency

A dental abscess is a pocket of infection that can spread rapidly into the jaw, neck, or even the airway if untreated. Warning signs include visible swelling around the jaw or neck, fever, difficulty swallowing, and a persistent foul taste. If you experience difficulty breathing or swallowing alongside dental swelling, go to the nearest emergency hospital immediately — this is life-threatening. For abscess without systemic symptoms, call your dentist the same day.

Broken or Cracked Tooth

A cracked tooth that causes sharp pain when biting likely has a crack reaching the nerve. Rinse with warm water, apply a cold compress on the outside of the cheek to reduce swelling, and see a dentist the same day. Do not attempt to bite on that side. If a piece of the tooth has broken off, bring it to your appointment — it may be possible to bond it back.

Lost Filling or Crown

A lost filling leaves the exposed dentine extremely sensitive. Temporary dental cement is available at pharmacies in Lagos and can be used as a short-term cover. Do not use superglue. Book an appointment to have it properly restored as soon as possible — exposed teeth can decay quickly.
